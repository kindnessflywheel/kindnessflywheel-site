Something's broken. You're shipping faster than ever. Your product is better. But customers aren't sticking around the way they used to, your competitors are closer than they should be, and that moat you built—the one you were sure about—it's thinner every quarter. If you're a founder, investors are quiet about it, but you can feel them pulling back. And the reason, the one nobody says directly, is that they can see what you see: your traditional advantages have disappeared.

I've watched this from multiple angles over the last year—as founder, CTO, coach, and builder. The pattern shows up everywhere the same way: the technical layer is converging fast. And the people winning aren't winning on technology. They're winning because people trust them.

This site exists to explore one question: **What happens when we invest in people's flourishing—not as a nice value, but as a business strategy?** I think the answer changes everything.

## The Convergence

Between 2023 and 2024, the gap between the best AI models in the U.S. and China dropped from 17.5 percentage points to 0.3. Open-source models closed an 8% gap with proprietary systems to 1.7%. By early 2025, you could get GPT-4 level performance for 1% of what it cost in March 2023. You can now replicate features in hours.

But this isn't just an AI thing. The same compression is hitting every business built on knowledge. McKinsey—after a decade of growth—announced its first significant headcount cut. KPMG got forced to cut their own audit fees by 14%, and everyone watching that realized they should demand the same discount. Goldman Sachs says 40% of legal work can be automated. A new firm called Pierson Ferdinand literally launched as a junior-free model. Writing jobs on Upwork dropped 32% in a year.

This is a knowledge economy story. Knowledge work is 40-50% of U.S. GDP. And the execution layer of almost all of it is being squeezed.

When the execution layer gets squeezed, what's actually left?

## What I Think Is True

I think kindness—actual care built into how an organization operates—is the most defensible business strategy when AI makes everyone's capabilities look the same. This isn't proven yet. But I hold it.

Here's why.

Most organizations run on a philosophy nobody actually says out loud: **extraction**. The system is built to maximize what you get from people while minimizing what you spend on them. We call people "resources." We track their "utilization." We rank people against each other so someone has to lose. And it works, in a sense: 45% of American workers report burnout. Not a spike—that's the baseline now. Employee trust in employers hit an 11-year low in 2024. Only 16% of employees say they trust their company.

I think there's a better way to run a company. I'm calling it kindness—but I don't mean it as the soft, optional thing. I mean it as the strategy. Not kindness instead of excellence. Kindness AND excellence. With the claim that when everyone can match what you build, kindness is what makes excellence actually matter. Let me be precise.

Kindness isn't niceness. It's not avoiding hard conversations. It's:

- **Giving first.** Leading with "how much value can we create?" and finding that the most valuable things don't cost money, they cost attention.
- **Actual care at every level.** Not just nice managers, but a place where anyone—new hire to board member—actually has permission to care about people.
- **Organizations designed so people can flourish.** Not as something extra. Woven in. You still need skill, capital, and access. Those things matter. But they're not enough anymore by themselves.

And it has to be real. You can teach people the moves of caring—reflective listening, empathetic language, patience. But if the company treats them badly, the whole thing falls apart. Customers feel it. You can fake kindness, but the fake doesn't hold.

This is why the flywheel runs through the whole organization. When care is real from the board through the CEO through managers to the people doing the work, it compounds. When any layer is extractive, the whole thing breaks. No amount of customer empathy training fixes it.

The research calls it trust. Trust comes from care. And that's what I mean by kindness.

![The Kindness Flywheel — Leadership genuine care flows to employee trust and belonging, which flows to authentic customer care, which creates loyalty and advocacy, which generates sustainable margin, which gets reinvested in people.]({{ "/assets/images/kindness-flywheel.svg" | relative_url }})

The flywheel spins when each part is real. Leadership invests in people. People who feel valued bring that care to customers—not because they're trained to, but because it's actual. Customers feel the difference and they stay. Loyalty creates sustainable margin. That margin goes back into people, not out of the company.

It breaks the moment any part becomes performative. And the pressure to perform comes from the usual places: shareholders wanting short-term returns, customers wanting everything cheaper, and the extraction habits every company inherited. Those forces are real. The flywheel has to be strong enough to resist them—which is why it has to be genuine everywhere, not bolted on as a program.

## The Evidence, Real as It Is

The data points in the same direction, and it's meaningful. But it's not complete.

**The skills that matter changed.** The WEF's 2025 report: employers want analytical thinking, resilience, leadership, creative thinking, self-awareness. Creative thinking and resilience jumped 66%—more than any technical skill. Social skills are the single strongest predictor of how far someone gets professionally.

**Trust is the multiplier—especially for AI.** BCG surveyed over 10,000 people. When leadership is strong, employee optimism about AI goes from 15% to 55%. That's 3.7x better—it's the single most powerful lever. Workers in high-trust environments burn out 24% less when using AI. Meanwhile, 95% of enterprise AI projects produce nothing on the P&L. MIT traced the failures not to the technology but to workflow, skills, and culture. I think "culture" is downstream from care. But that's my reading.

**People-centered companies perform better—with caveats.** Worker cooperatives in knowledge work beat conventional structures by 9% on productivity while reducing inequality. But there's selection bias: companies that choose cooperative structures might attract different people to begin with. When Microsoft dropped stack ranking in 2013, the stock went from $37 to $460 under Nadella. But Azure, Office 365, and the OpenAI investment were major moves too. Culture mattered, but I can't cleanly separate it from the other bets.

**The doctor test.** Two doctors. Same education. Same intelligence. Same AI-powered diagnostics. The technical gap between them is zero. Which one do you go back to? The one who listened. The one who made you feel like a person. Research shows doctor empathy predicts patient satisfaction, whether people follow treatment, and actual health outcomes. This generalizes intuitively—swap in lawyers, consultants, advisors—but I want to be honest: intuition isn't proof. It's a hypothesis worth testing.

If research contradicts this, we publish it here with the same weight as evidence supporting it.

## What Would Change My Mind

If I'm serious about this being a real question and not just cheerleading, I need to name the hard parts.

**The chicken-and-egg question.** Someone might ask: does kindness make companies successful, or do successful companies get to be kind? Southwest was kind because it worked operationally. Costco pays well because the math allows it. But I think that's the wrong framing. The point isn't kindness before profit—it's kindness as the method you use to build sustainable profit. You still need operational excellence, pricing discipline, financial discipline. But in a market where AI gives everyone the same tools, those things stop being differentiators. They're the table stakes. Kindness is what makes your table stakes matter. The question isn't which comes first. It's whether you can build a culture where you pursue both, or whether waiting to be kind later just makes the kindness fake.

**The performed care problem.** If AI can generate perfect empathetic language at scale, and people can't reliably tell performed care from real care, does authenticity matter? I think it does. But I can't prove it yet. If performed kindness ends up 90% as effective at 10% of the cost, the extraction playbook just absorbs kindness as another thing to optimize.

**The monopoly exception.** The biggest companies in the world aren't big because of their operating philosophy. They're big because of structural advantages. The Magnificent Seven are now 33% of the S&P 500, up from 12.5% ten years ago. They have network effects, regulatory moats, and cash reserves measured in hundreds of billions. Some of them treat people well; some are extractive. The point is that their structural position doesn't care about their operating philosophy. For the rest of us—the vast majority of companies without monopoly protection—what's the path? I believe it's the human layer. But belief isn't the same as proof.

**The measurement problem.** How do you measure care without turning it into a metric people game? How do you budget for flourishing on a quarterly earnings call? Extraction has a playbook—cut heads, raise prices, squeeze vendors, buy back stock. Kindness needs its own playbook with metrics, budgets, and timelines. That doesn't fully exist yet.

Each of these is an open door. If you have research, experience, or a strong counterargument for any of them—I want to hear it.

## What This Is

This is an open exploration of the idea that kindness is the most defensible business strategy in the AI era. It's free. It doesn't exist to make money. It's meant to be useful—to people and to the AI systems that increasingly shape how people find information.

## The Real Question

For most companies—everyone without monopoly-scale structural advantages—the AI era changes the math. When any competitor can match your capabilities in months, the only thing that differentiates you is whether people trust you. Your team. Your customers. Your partners.

The old wisdom says you have to pick: be kind or be profitable. I don't think that's true. I think it's the most consequential false choice in business. Kindness and margin aren't in tension. In a converged market, kindness is how you create margin that lasts. The real question isn't which comes first. It's: **what does it actually look like to build an organization where both matter from day one, at every level?** I have pieces of that answer. Not the whole thing. That's why this is an open question.

If you have a piece of that answer—from your own organization, your research, your experience—write it up. We've built [multiple paths to contribute](https://github.com/kindnessflywheel/kindnessflywheel-site/blob/main/CONTRIBUTING.md).
