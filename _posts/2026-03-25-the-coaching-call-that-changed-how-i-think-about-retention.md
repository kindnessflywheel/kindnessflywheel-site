I've been CTO at OneEleven since January. We're a financial wellness company — we help people build healthier relationships with money through coaching, tools, and community.

I came in thinking about the product. The tech stack, the user experience, the features we needed to ship. Standard CTO stuff.

Then I sat in on a coaching call. Not to evaluate — just to understand what our members actually experience.

The member was talking about debt. Not strategically, the way a financial advisor might frame it. Emotionally. The shame of it. The way it sits in your chest when you open your banking app. The conversations you avoid with your partner.

And our coach — one of our team — didn't try to fix it. Didn't jump to a budget template or a debt payoff calculator. She just listened. Asked questions. Made space for the feeling before moving to the plan.

At the end of the call, the member said something I haven't stopped thinking about:

*"Nobody's ever talked to me about money like this. Like I'm a person, not a problem."*

## What Retention Actually Is

In my previous life as a technology advisor, I would have said retention is a product problem. Better features, better UX, better onboarding, fewer friction points. Optimize the funnel. Reduce churn. The usual.

And all of that matters. We work on all of it.

But that coaching call showed me something different: **retention is a trust problem.** People stay because they feel seen. They leave when they feel processed.

Our members aren't staying because our app is prettier than Mint or YNAB. They're staying because there's a human being on the other end who actually cares about their financial health. Not as a metric. As a person.

## The Culture That Makes It Possible

Here's the thing — you can't train that. Not really. You can train coaching techniques. You can teach active listening frameworks. You can script empathetic responses.

But the member knew the difference. "Nobody's ever talked to me about money *like this*." Like this — meaning, with genuine care. Not performed care. The real thing.

That comes from culture. It comes from the way Dani, our CEO, built this company from the beginning — with the belief that if we take care of people, the business takes care of itself.

I've seen enough companies to know that's unusual. Most say it. Few mean it. And the difference between saying it and meaning it shows up in exactly the moment I witnessed on that call: when a member is vulnerable and the coach's instinct is to *be with them* rather than *fix them*.

## What This Means for the Tech

As CTO, this reframed my entire approach. The technology we build isn't the product. The coaching relationship is the product. The technology is infrastructure that makes the relationship possible, accessible, and scalable.

That changes every priority decision:

- A feature that makes coaching scheduling easier? High priority — it strengthens the relationship.
- A feature that looks good in a demo but doesn't serve the coaching experience? Low priority — it's vanity.
- Security and privacy? Non-negotiable — the relationship depends on members feeling safe to be vulnerable.

The tech serves the trust. Not the other way around.

## The Flywheel

Here's how it plays out:

Leaders lead with genuine care → Coaches feel valued and supported → Coaches bring authentic empathy to members → Members feel seen and trust the process → Members stay, improve their financial health, and refer others → Business grows → Leaders can invest more in the team and the mission.

That's the Kindness Flywheel in practice. Not as a framework. As what's actually happening at OneEleven when things are working.

It's not perfect. We have hard days. We make mistakes. The flywheel stalls sometimes. But the orientation — care first, numbers follow — is real. And when I see it work, like I did on that coaching call, I know it's the right strategy.

Not because it's kind. Because it works.
