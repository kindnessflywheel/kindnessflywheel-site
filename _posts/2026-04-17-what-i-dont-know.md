Last month I published [The Race to the Mean](/2026/03/28/the-race-to-the-mean/) and made a big claim: that kindness is the most defensible business strategy in the age of AI. I believe that. But believing something and being able to prove it are different things, and I'd rather be honest about the gaps than pretend they don't exist.

What follows is a list of questions I genuinely don't know the answer to. Each one could be its own article — or series. If you read one of these and think *I have something to say about that*, that's the point. [Here's how to contribute](https://github.com/kindnessflywheel/kindnessflywheel-site/blob/main/CONTRIBUTOR_GUIDE.md).

---

## Strategy

**1. Is kindness a cause of performance, or a consequence of margin?** Southwest was kind because it was operationally excellent first. Costco pays well because its unit economics allow it. Maybe you earn the right to be kind by winning the hard way first. I can't yet separate whether care drives results or whether results create the space for care. Someone needs to trace the sequencing in real companies.

**2. Does extraction work indefinitely at monopoly scale?** The Magnificent Seven are the most extractive and the most dominant. If extraction works when you have structural advantages — and those companies represent a third of the S&P 500 — then the kindness thesis only applies to the long tail. Is that true? Or do monopolies eventually pay a price for extraction that just hasn't come due yet? History says they fall, but the mechanism isn't always kindness-related.

**3. What's the capital allocation playbook for a kindness-operated company?** Extraction has a playbook: cut headcount, raise prices, squeeze vendors, buy back stock. If I'm a PE firm or a public-company CEO and you've convinced me, what do I *do* on Monday? "Care genuinely" isn't an operating instruction. I need budgets, metrics, timelines, and a board deck. Nobody's written that yet.

**4. Can customers actually distinguish authentic care from performed care at scale?** I asserted that customers feel the difference between genuine and manufactured empathy. But plenty of research suggests they can't — especially in asynchronous channels, at scale, and when AI generates the language. If performed kindness is 90% as effective at 10% of the cost, the extractive playbook just absorbs kindness as another optimizable input. Who has evidence either way?

**5. Does kindness work in B2B procurement contexts?** The doctor analogy is compelling for consumer relationships. But B2B buyers are procurement departments running RFPs on price and SLA. Care doesn't show up on the scorecard. Is there evidence that trust-based relationships win in enterprise sales, or is that a story sellers tell themselves?

## Education

**6. What happens to the career ladder when AI normalizes technical skills?** If the execution layer compresses and human skills become the differentiator, what does professional advancement look like? Do we promote people for empathy? How do you credential that? The entire structure of professional education assumes technical mastery is the path up. What replaces it?

**7. Can you teach kindness in professional training, or is it dispositional?** Business schools teach case studies and frameworks. Medical schools teach bedside manner through standardized patients. But is genuine care — the kind that survives organizational pressure — something you can develop in a classroom? Or does professional training select for it and then either preserve or destroy it?

**8. What does "kindness" look like in non-Western business cultures?** Everything in the inaugural post is grounded in American and European business norms. But care, trust, and relational commerce have deep roots in other traditions — *guanxi* in Chinese business, *ubuntu* in South African philosophy, *omotenashi* in Japanese service culture. Is the kindness hypothesis a Western rediscovery of something other cultures never lost? Someone with cross-cultural experience should write this.

---

## Technology

**9. What's the role of AI in amplifying genuine care — and can it undermine it?** AI can generate perfectly empathetic language at scale. Does that make authentic care more or less valuable? If an AI assistant drafts a compassionate email that a human sends without reading closely, is that kindness or performance? Where's the line between AI-augmented care and AI-replaced care?

**10. How do you measure care without turning it into a KPI that gets gamed?** The moment you put empathy on a scorecard, you create incentives to perform it rather than practice it. NPS was supposed to measure delight and became a harassment tool. How do you build systems that track the health of human relationships without destroying them through measurement? This is a design problem someone should solve.

**11. Is there a minimum viable trust level below which the flywheel can't start?** The thesis assumes a flywheel: care generates trust, trust generates performance, performance generates capacity for more care. But flywheels need activation energy. In a low-trust organization or market, is there a threshold you have to cross before the cycle starts? What does it take to get there? And how much does it cost?

---

## Practice

**12. What does a kindness-operated organization look like in year 1 versus year 5?** I can describe the philosophy. I can't yet describe the trajectory. What's hard in the first year that gets easier? What looks easy at first and turns out to be the real challenge? I need case studies that track the arc, not just the snapshot.

**13. How do you maintain authenticity as you scale?** A ten-person company can be kind through founder behavior. A thousand-person company needs systems. At what point does codifying care start to feel corporate and hollow? What organizational structures preserve genuine care through growth? Every company that's tried this has a story about where it almost broke.

**14. The trust research has a survivorship problem.** Companies with high-trust cultures that also succeed get studied and write books. Companies with high-trust cultures that got eaten by ruthless competitors don't. I cited BCG and MIT on culture driving AI adoption — but "culture" in those reports usually means change management capacity, not kindness. Who's tracking the kind companies that failed, and what can we learn from them?

**15. What does kindness look like when you have to do hard things?** Layoffs, performance management, shutting down a product, saying no to a customer. The critique of kindness-as-strategy is that it falls apart when the decision is painful. I don't think that's true — I think kind organizations make hard decisions *better* — but I need stories from people who've done it, not just my intuition.

---

## Meta

**16. How do we avoid the unfalsifiability trap?** A sharp critic pointed out that the kindness hypothesis as stated can't lose: any failure can be attributed to "not real kindness" and any extractive success can be attributed to structural advantages. That's a problem. What would *disprove* this hypothesis? I have some ideas, but the community should define the falsification criteria together.

**17. What does the head-to-head study look like?** The strongest evidence would be a matched cohort study — same sector, same size, same capital structure — where kindness-operated companies are tracked against extractive ones over five or more years on revenue growth, retention, and margin. Has anyone designed that study? Could we?

**18. Is "kindness" the right word?** The research uses "trust," "psychological safety," "human-centered leadership." I chose "kindness" because it's simpler and more direct. But it carries baggage — softness, naiveté, weakness. Does the word help or hurt? Is there a better frame that preserves the meaning without the connotation?

---

Every one of these questions represents something I believe but can't prove, or something I suspect but haven't investigated. If you have a piece of the answer — data, a story, a counterargument, a lived experience — [write it up](https://github.com/kindnessflywheel/kindnessflywheel-site/blob/main/CONTRIBUTOR_GUIDE.md). The hypothesis gets stronger through challenge, not agreement.
