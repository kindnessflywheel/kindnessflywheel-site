I've spent twenty-five years in technology. I've watched entire skill sets become obsolete in five-year cycles. I've seen brilliant engineers become irrelevant because they couldn't communicate. I've seen mediocre technologists thrive because people trusted them.

Now I'm watching AI accelerate this pattern to the point where it breaks the old model entirely.

And I keep coming back to the same thought: if I were advising a young person today — my son Avery, or anyone starting out — the most important thing I could teach them has nothing to do with technology.

## What AI Commoditizes

Let's be honest about what's happening:

**Knowledge** — AI already knows more than any human expert in any field. Not better judgment, but more raw knowledge. And the judgment gap is closing.

**Technical skill** — AI can write code, design systems, analyze data, and produce professional-quality work across dozens of domains. The skill floor has been raised to "competent" for everyone.

**Productivity** — With AI augmentation, a single person can do what used to require a team. The output gap between individuals is narrowing.

**Communication quality** — AI can write clear emails, polished reports, and compelling presentations. The baseline quality of professional communication is rising for everyone.

If your competitive advantage was knowing more, doing more, or producing better artifacts than the next person — that advantage is disappearing.

## What AI Can't Replace

Here's what's left. These are the skills that survive commoditization because they're fundamentally human:

**Empathy** — The ability to actually understand what someone else is experiencing. Not simulate understanding. Not produce the right words. Actually feel what another person feels and respond to it authentically.

**Care** — Giving a damn about outcomes that don't directly benefit you. Staying late not because you have to, but because someone is counting on you. Following up not because it's in your OKRs, but because you said you would.

**Compassion** — The willingness to sit with someone else's difficulty without trying to fix it, optimize it, or escape it. Being present when it's uncomfortable.

**Communication** — Not writing — AI can write. The human kind of communication: reading a room, knowing when to speak and when to listen, delivering hard truths with care, building consensus through genuine dialogue.

**Creativity and imagination** — Not content generation — AI does that. The ability to see what doesn't exist yet. To connect ideas across domains in ways that surprise. To envision futures that nobody has described.

## Not Soft Skills — THE Skills

I need to push back on the framing that these are "soft skills" — as if they're supplements to the real, hard skills of business.

In a commoditized world, these aren't additions to the playbook. They ARE the playbook.

If every candidate for a job has AI-augmented technical capability (and they will), the hiring decision comes down to: Who do I trust? Who will my team trust? Who will our customers trust?

The answer is the person who genuinely cares. Who communicates with empathy. Who creates things nobody else imagined. Who shows up when it matters.

## What I'm Learning From My Own Kid

My son Avery is growing up in a world I couldn't have imagined at his age. The tools available to him are extraordinary. The challenges facing him are equally extraordinary.

What I want him to know isn't how to use AI — he'll figure that out, same as his generation figured out smartphones and social media. What I want him to know is:

The person who remembers your name, asks how your day was, and actually listens to the answer — that person will always be valuable. In any economy. Under any technological regime. Because that person makes other people feel seen, and feeling seen is the thing humans will never stop needing.

That's not career advice in the traditional sense. But I think it's the most practical thing I can teach him.

## For Leaders

If you're leading a team or an organization, this has implications for how you hire, how you train, and how you evaluate performance.

Stop over-indexing on technical skills that AI will commoditize within five years. Start over-indexing on:

- Can this person build trust with colleagues and clients?
- Do they show genuine care for outcomes beyond their own success?
- Can they communicate with empathy under pressure?
- Do they bring creative insight that surprises the team?
- Are they someone others want to work with?

These aren't secondary criteria. They're primary. They're what will make your organization defensible when every competitor has the same technical capability you do.

Raise them kind. Hire them kind. Promote them kind. Not because it's nice — because it's the strategy.
